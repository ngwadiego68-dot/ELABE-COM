1. Definition and Calculation of Heat ($Q$)

Definition of Heat: Heat is the **transfer of thermal energy** between two objects or systems due to a **temperature difference**. Heat is energy *in transit* and is measured in **Joules (J)**. The net flow is always from the hotter object to the colder object.

Formula for Heat Transfer (No Phase Change)

The amount of heat ($Q$) required to change the temperature ($\Delta \theta$) of a substance of mass ($m$) is given by:

$$Q = mc\Delta \theta$$ Where: $m$ = mass ($\text{kg}$), $c$ = specific heat capacity ($\text{J}/\text{kg}^\circ \text{C}$), $\Delta \theta$ = temperature change ($\text{C}^\circ$).

2. Heat Capacity and Specific Heat Capacity

Specific Heat Capacity ($c$)

Definition: The heat energy required to raise the temperature of **$1 \text{ kg}$** of a substance by **$1^\circ \text{C}$** (or $1 \text{ K}$).

S.I. Unit: $\text{Joules per kilogram per degree Celsius} (\mathbf{J}/\mathbf{kg}^\circ \mathbf{C})$.

Heat Capacity ($C$)

Definition: The total heat energy required to raise the temperature of the **entire mass** of a substance by **$1^\circ \text{C}$** (or $1 \text{ K}$).

Formula: $C = \frac{Q}{\Delta \theta}$ or $C = mc$

S.I. Unit: $\text{Joules per degree Celsius} (\mathbf{J}/\mathbf{^\circ C})$.

Example and Solving:

A $2.0 \text{ kg}$ block of Aluminium ($\text{c} = 900 \text{ J}/\text{kg}^\circ \text{C}$) is heated from $25^\circ \text{C}$ to $55^\circ \text{C}$. Calculate the heat absorbed ($Q$).

Data: $m = 2.0 \text{ kg}$, $c = 900 \text{ J}/\text{kg}^\circ \text{C}$, $\Delta \theta = 55 - 25 = 30^\circ \text{C}$.

Calculation:

$$Q = mc\Delta \theta$$ $$Q = (2.0) \times (900) \times (30)$$ $$Q = \mathbf{54,000 \text{ J}}$$

3. Conductor and Insulator

Heat Conductor

Definition: A material that allows heat energy to pass through it easily and quickly (e.g., metals like Copper, Silver, and Aluminium). Conductors typically have **low specific heat capacities** and are used in cooking utensils and radiators.

Heat Insulator

Definition: A material that does not allow heat energy to pass through it easily; it is a poor conductor of heat (e.g., wood, plastic, wool, and air). Insulators typically have **high specific heat capacities** and are used in clothing and building walls.

4. The Thermometer and its Types üå°Ô∏è

Definition and Principle

A **thermometer** is a device used to measure temperature. It works on the principle that the physical properties of a material (like volume, resistance, or color) change **uniformly** with temperature.

Types of Thermometers (Common in Cameroon Standards)

  • **Mercury-in-Glass Thermometer:**

    Uses the expansion of mercury as its thermometric property. Commonly used in laboratories and for measuring high temperatures. **Range:** $-10^\circ \text{C}$ to $150^\circ \text{C}$ or more.

  • **Alcohol-in-Glass Thermometer:**

    Uses the expansion of coloured alcohol. Suitable for measuring very low temperatures (e.g., in cold rooms) because alcohol freezes at a much lower temperature than mercury. **Range:** $-100^\circ \text{C}$ to $80^\circ \text{C}$.

  • **Clinical Thermometer:**

    A specialized mercury or digital thermometer used to measure human body temperature. It has a **constriction** just above the bulb to prevent the liquid from falling immediately, allowing time for reading. **Range:** $35^\circ \text{C}$ to $43^\circ \text{C}$.
