Organic Chemistry: Structure, Series, and Reactions 🧪

1. Introduction & Core Definitions

Organic Chemistry: The study of carbon compounds, which involves all compounds except simple oxides of carbon (CO, $CO_2$), carbonates ($Na_2CO_3$), and cyanides (KCN).

Organic Compound: A compound containing the element Carbon, usually bonded to Hydrogen (forming hydrocarbons) and often to Oxygen, Nitrogen, or Halogens.

Hydrocarbon: An organic compound composed **only** of Carbon (C) and Hydrogen (H) atoms.

2. The Homologous Series

A Homologous Series is a family of organic compounds sharing the same functional group and the same general formula**. Each successive member differs by a constant **-CH₂-** unit (the methylene group).

Properties change gradually (e.g., boiling point increases) as the molecular mass increases down the series.

3. Alkanes: Saturated Hydrocarbons

Alkanes are saturated (containing only single bonds) and are relatively unreactive. They form the basis of the petroleum industry.

Alkane & Alkyl Groups

Alkane General Formula: $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+2}$
Alkyl Group General Formula: $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+1}$ (Formed when one H atom is removed from an alkane, e.g., Methane $\rightarrow$ Methyl group)

Reactions of Alkanes

A. Combustion (Burning) 🔥

Alkanes are excellent fuels. Complete combustion** (excess oxygen) produces carbon dioxide and water:

CH4 + 2O2 →CO2 + 2H2O

Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces Carbon Monoxide (CO) or Carbon (Soot).

B. Substitution (Halogenation)

Requires UV light or high temperatures. A Halogen atom replaces a Hydrogen atom (free radical mechanism):

CH4 + Cl2 + (UV light) → CH3Cl + HCl

4. Alkenes: Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Alkenes contain at least one C=C double bond, making them Unsaturated and much more reactive than alkanes.

Key Alkene Characteristics

  • General Formula: CnH2n
  • Simplest Member: Ethene (C2H4)
  • Primary Reaction Type: Addition reactions (the double bond breaks, and new atoms are added to the Carbon chain).

Reactions of Alkenes

A. Addition Reactions (The Test for Unsaturation)

Alkenes decolorize Bromine water (orange-brown) instantly, which is the chemical test to distinguish them from alkanes.

C2H4 + Br2 →C2H4Br2 (1,2-dibromoethane, colorless)

B. Polymerization

Small alkene molecules (monomers) join together under heat, pressure, and catalyst to form very long polymer chains (e.g., polyethene from ethene).

$$\mathbf{n \cdot (\text{C}_2\text{H}_4) \longrightarrow (-\text{CH}_2-\text{CH}_2- )_n}$$

5. Alcohols ($\text{ROH}$)

Alcohols are organic compounds where a Hydroxyl group ($\mathbf{-OH}$) is bonded to an alkyl group ($\text{R}$).

Types of Alcohols (Classification)

Alcohols are classified based on the number of alkyl groups attached to the carbon bearing the $\text{-OH}$ group:

  1. Primary ($1^\circ$): The carbon attached to the $\text{-OH}$ is bonded to only one other carbon atom (e.g., Ethanol, $\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH}$).
  2. Secondary ($2^\circ$): The carbon attached to the $\text{-OH}$ is bonded to two other carbon atoms.
  3. Tertiary ($3^\circ$): The carbon attached to the $\text{-OH}$ is bonded to three other carbon atoms.

6. Key Concepts and Industrial Processes

A. Isomerism and its Types

Isomers: Compounds having the **same molecular formula** but **different arrangement** of atoms in space. Isomerism explains why the same formula can represent multiple distinct substances.

The main types encountered at this level are:

  1. **Structural Isomerism:** Isomers have different connectivity (different structures/bond arrangement).
    • *Example:* **Butane** ($\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$) exists as a straight chain (n-Butane) and a branched chain (2-Methylpropane).
  2. **Geometrical Isomerism (cis-trans):** Occurs in Alkenes due to restricted rotation around the $\text{C}=\text{C}$ double bond.

B. Cracking (The Oil Industry) 🏭

Cracking: The process of breaking down **long-chain, high molecular mass hydrocarbons** (which are less useful) into **smaller, more useful hydrocarbons** (like petrol/gasoline and alkenes) using heat and catalysts.

$$\mathbf{\text{Large Alkane} \longrightarrow \text{Smaller Alkane} + \text{Alkene}}$$

Cracking is a major industrial source of alkenes, which are needed for polymerization.

C. Saponification (Soap Making) 🧼

Saponification: The alkaline hydrolysis (breaking down with a strong base like NaOH or KOH) of **fats or oils** to produce **soap** (a salt of a fatty acid) and **glycerol** (propan-1,2,3-triol).

It is the core chemical reaction in traditional soap manufacturing.

$$\mathbf{\text{Fat} (\text{Ester}) + \text{Base} (\text{NaOH}) \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} \text{Soap} + \text{Glycerol}}$$
